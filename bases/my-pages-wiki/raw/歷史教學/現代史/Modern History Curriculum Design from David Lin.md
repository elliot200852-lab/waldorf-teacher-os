Topic: Bridging History and Humanity: A 9th Grade Modern History Curriculum Design
Speaker: David Lin (Lin Hsin-Hung), Ci-Xin Waldorf High School, Taiwan
Dear Colleagues and Educational Partners,
Hello. I am David Lin from Ci-Xin Waldorf High School in Taiwan. It is my honor to share with you the design and practice of my 9th-grade Modern History Main Lesson.
Facing ninth graders—adolescents in the throes of puberty, filled with internal storms and deep longing—I know that history class cannot simply be a dry recitation of dates. Below, I will dissect my curriculum from four dimensions, illustrating how I use this course to accompany students in seeing history, and through it, seeing themselves.
1. Design Philosophy: Shifting from "Knowledge Accumulation" to "Humanistic Awakening"
I often ask myself: Why teach Modern History? To me, history is not merely knowledge to be learned, but "a process experienced, endured, and recreated by human beings."
The Rationale: Ninth-grade students are at a critical period where their speculative powers are budding and their values are being reorganized. They begin to question authority and are curious about the world. Therefore, my curriculum design must respond to their inner needs. This is not just a history class; it is an "awakening of humanistic consciousness."
The Logic: I set the core theme as "How Man Becomes Modern." We start from the light of reason in the Enlightenment, move through the passion and chaos of the French Revolution, walk into the mechanical clamor of the Industrial Revolution, and finally face the trauma of the World Wars. I want students to see not cold events, but the "human struggle and light" within history. For example, when discussing Napoleon, we don't just look at his military exploits; we discuss, "How much freedom did he sacrifice for order?" and "In driving history forward, was he also being driven by it?"
2. Utilizing AI: It is the Material, I am the Soul
Facing the vast complexity of modern history, I do not reject technology. On the contrary, I view AI as an "External Knowledge System" that acts as an extension of my teaching wisdom.
AI as an Efficient Assistant: I use AI to handle structural work. I input themes (e.g., "Industrial Revolution," "Napoleon") and ask it to generate lecture skeletons, key concepts, and historical positioning. This has drastically reduced my preparation time—sometimes to one-fifth of what it was—allowing me to focus my energy on pedagogical design. However, I constantly remind myself: AI provides the "material," but I must endow it with "soul." AI can list Napoleon’s battles, but it cannot convey the "temperature of the human experience."
Transformation and Infusing Life: Previously, I struggled to find suitable supplementary reading materials. Now, I instruct AI to produce "Deep Research" tailored to the context of my 9th-grade design. I then verify and interpret this AI-generated content, adding emotion and narrative. For instance, when AI mentions Napoleon’s failed expedition to Russia, I supplement it with descriptions of the bitter cold, the food shortages, and the soldiers' fear, helping students understand it was not just a strategic error, but a test of "the limits of human will." The key to not being controlled by AI is that I retain the power of "Meaning-Making," ensuring reading materials integrate a holistic view rather than just filling gaps in knowledge.
3. Note-Taking Design: Transforming from "Recording" to "Thinking"
To prevent students from getting lost in the flood of information, I adapted the Cornell Notes system to design a specific format: “Modern History Main Lesson 2025: Student Note Format.” This provides a scaffold for self-directed learning and free writing in their main lesson workbooks.
The "Double-Page Spread" Design:
Left Page (Objective/Guided): Used for recording the lesson's main axis, key events, and figures (e.g., technological developments of the Industrial Revolution). It includes simple prompts to help students summarize or form inquiry questions.
Right Page (Subjective/Free Writing): This is space for reflection. Here, students synthesize reading materials, class content, and their own questions. They write down their perspectives, such as: "How do the human desire for exploration and the desire for power run parallel?" or "How does freedom move from an idea to an institution?"
Cultivation of Faculties:
Internalization: I implement "Silent Note-Taking Time" in class, using a bell as a signal. This allows students to settle their thoughts in the moment, transforming external knowledge into internal understanding, and prevents the teacher from lecturing incessantly.
Process over Result: Notes constitute a large portion of my assessment. I tell students: "The process of thinking is more important than the answer." I hope that through their notes, they learn not just history, but the ability to organize information and establish a viewpoint.
4. Leveraging AI for Empathy and Connection: Achieving Previously Impossible Designs
In the past, my Main Lessons were often limited to one-way narration due to time constraints in preparation. Now, through dialogue with AI, I can easily prepare abundant supplementary materials (scripts, poems, manifestos, comparison tables), allowing for multi-dimensional capacity building:
Cultivating Empathy and Perspective-Taking: For example, I asked AI to write four scripts from different perspectives (e.g., “Dawn in the Smog”), allowing students to role-play factory owners, female workers, and displaced farmers. In an era that only discussed "production value," I let students experience the "human cost" and class contradictions of the Industrial Revolution through drama. These performances then replaced standard teacher lectures.
Cultivating Value Judgment and Critical Thinking: AI helped design an activity called "If You Had a Factory," where students calculated profits versus wages. They had to choose between being an "exploitative boss" or a "bankrupt philanthropist." We also introduced excerpts from Marx's Communist Manifesto so students could understand why people back then cried out for revolution, rather than viewing it merely as a political label.
Cultivating Insight into Past and Present: I deliberately juxtaposed Robert Owen (the 19th-century social reformer) with contemporary social movements (like Taiwan's Sunflower Student Movement) and current AI ethical issues. This bridges the "gap between ancient and modern" often found in traditional history teaching, helping students understand that history is fluid. The fight for labor rights a century ago and today's youth pursuit of social justice are, in fact, a continuation of the same force.
In Conclusion
My hope for this course is to provide students with a Lamp and a Mirror.
The Lamp (AI assistance and structured notes) helps them efficiently illuminate the complex context of history.
The Mirror (Reflection and Humanistic Historical View) allows them to see themselves in the struggles of historical figures.
When a student writes in their notes, "Napoleon, while pushing history forward, was also being pushed by history," they are actually practicing understanding themselves: We are all within history—pushing, enduring, and recreating it.
This is the gift I, as a Waldorf teacher, hope to bring to the children.
