Meeting Charming Taiwan Part Ⅰ
This is a bear, a bear from Taiwan. Oh, Taiwan! Yeah, you know that island floating between Japan and the Philippines that looks kind of like a sweet potato? Doesn't ring a bell? All right, what about bubble tea? Yes! I knew it. Bubble tea Always works.
Well, this particular bear just happens to be boarding a flight back to the home of bubble tea and himself. Want to see what it's like? Then let's go!

Food is the essence of life, isn't that right? To thoroughly understand Taiwan, we've got to start from the nutritious and delicious food. PH-OU-DE, food. Now we all know about bubble tea, milk tea, and black tapioca balls, also known as bubbles or boba. This is the signature drink of Taiwan. It's not just bubble tea, though. Tea drinking has been in our culture since forever. Tea houses used to be the center of social interaction.What can add more to a nice chat than a cup of tea? 

Yet as sealing machines came along in the 1980s, these drinks evolved into a more portable form and thus gave rise to shaken tea shops. Nowadays, everyone on the streets in Taiwan has a tea in their hand.
Now that we're all about this food business, we've got to take you to the night markets. Night markets are the best experience for local food: fried chicken, oyster omelets, stinky tofu, and that's what I'm talking about! 
While you're busy stuffing your face with all this youngness, don't forget to check out all the Chinese characters around you. Taiwan is one of the few places left that still uses traditional Chinese.

You must think these characters look complicated as hell, right? We think so too? You know how long it takes me to write my name? Think God I'm not famous and we need to do signatures and stuff.

Anyway, all the signs in the night market exhibit the full glory of our characters. You see, these characters go way back. 3,000 years ago, they were written on turtle shells and the bones of other animals for oracles, so the ancient characters were also called Oracle bone script.

For some of our characters, you can tell the meaning of the word just by its appearance. This thing that looks like a mountain transformed into the character we now use to signify the word "mountain". This thing that looks like a river transformed into a character we now use to signify the word "river". You can tell, right? This looks like a mountain and this looks like a river.

Meeting Charming Taiwan Part Ⅲ
This multicultural environment is actually because of Taiwan's history and location. It's not just food, though; infrastructure as well. Our railways were laid down over a hundred years ago by the Japanese. What? Yeah, the Japanese. Then I mentioned that Japan ruled us before. They're not the only ones, either. The Ching dynasty of China, the Dutch, and the Spanish have taken over as well. That's why you can still hear certain places being called not-so-Mandarin names, like Zealandia or San Diego.
Having been influenced by different cultures, Taiwan is now super accepting of other cultures. Southeast Asian brothers and sisters can live or work here. In certain train stations, there's even more foreigners than locals. As a result, you can also see different places of worship in Taiwan. There are prayer rooms for Muslims in high-speed rail train stations.
And Taiwan's own religious culture has a ton of its own festivals. Taiwan's religious diversity index is the second highest in the world. You can see more religious buildings than convenience stores. Registered temples alone go up to 12,000, meaning that you can find a temple every 3 square kilometers. Consequently, the religious activity in Taiwan is spectacular.
Be sure to check out the Beehive fireworks or sky lanterns if you have a chance. Both events pray for peace, even though fireworks.
