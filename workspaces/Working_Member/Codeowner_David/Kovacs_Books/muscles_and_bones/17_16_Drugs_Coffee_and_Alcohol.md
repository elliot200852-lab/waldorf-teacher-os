# 16. Drugs, Coffee and Alcohol

We use plants for our food; they give starch, sugar, vitamins, fat, protein.
Other plants
are not so nourishing and would not feed us, but the
spices, for instance, make our food tasty: mustard, ginger, pepper. Then
there are also plants which we use for quite different reasons.
In South America there is a tree called cinconcha. Its bark has a ter-
ribly bitter taste. Nobody would want to have this bitter taste for plea-
sure, but from this bitter bark quinine is made, which is a medicine
against malaria. The plants not only give us food but also a great many
medicines. Just think how wonderful it is that for a human illness some-
where there is a plant which is the cure.
Poppies in a field here are just beautiful, red flowers; but in India and
China there is a white poppy from which a juice is pressed out. From this
poppy juice one can make a very special medicine called morphine. Be-
fore a surgeon operates on a person they are put into such a deep sleep
that they do not wake up or feel anything during the operation. Mor-
phine puts the patient into this deep sleep. It is a drug which can be
used as an anaesthetic.
For the people who have to be operated on morphine, made from
poppy juice, is a great blessing: it avoids the pain of the operation. But
every blessing can also be a curse. This poppy juice can also be made
into a white rubbery stuff: opium. Some people put this opium in pipes
and smoke it. And when they have taken a few puffs, they fall asleep and
they have the most wonderful dreams. The dreams are so beautiful that
when they awake they only long to go back to the dreams again. So they
cannot live without the dreams, without opium. But taken regularly
opium destroys the brain, and the people who have become addicted to
opium die after a few years. So the plant world gives us drugs which can
be a blessing or a curse.
All drugs are really poisons, but a poison is sometimes a medicine.
For example, aconite or monkshood is a plant whose root contains a very
strong poison. But for people who have a cold or flu coming on, a very
little of this aconite is an excellent medicine.
There are drugs or poisons which people take not as medicines, but
because they enjoy the harmful effect. It seems strange that anybody

could enjoy harming their body, but it really happens. For instance caf-
feine, which is found in coffee and tea, is such a poison. Caffeine is just
the opposite of opium: it makes you more awake. An Arab goatherd no-
ticed that when his goats had eaten berries from certain shrubs in the
evening, they would not lie down to sleep, but frisked about. And so first
Arabs, then Turks and later Europeans began to drink coffee to be more
alert and more awake.
But coffee only makes you more alert by making your heart beat
faster than it would beat if left to itself. Your heart may want to beat
slowly, because your whole body is tired, but coffee works like whipping
a tired horse. Of course it is bad for the heart. But people are more cruel
with their hearts than they would be with a horse, and so they drink cof-
fee.
Tea contains the same poison, caffeine, but not quite so strong. And
tea too drives the heart faster. And it does something else as well; it
makes us more talkative; it makes us talk just for the sake of talking. Cof-
fee is not quite the same - people who drink great quantities of coffee
want to talk only cleverly, but tea drinkers like to chatter whether it's
clever or not. Both coffee and tea are whips driving the heart faster than
it wants to go. Caffeine is also added to some soft drinks, such as Coca-
Cola or Red Bull.
And lastly I want to tell you of another poison which people drink:
alcohol. It is made by a process called fermentation in which a liquid
brew containing yeast breaks down starch and changes it to alcohol.
When we use yeast to make bread this is also a fermentation process. In
nature fermentation is really a decay process (for example the rotting of
over-ripe fruit) but we can control and make use of it. In fermentation
starch and sugar, the energy foods, are broken down and changed into
alcohol.
Alcohol is a very useful substance which is used for example in
hospitals to wipe things down because it quickly kills any germs which
might carry disease, and it has many other uses in laboratories. Of
course it is also the substance which is in wine, beer, whisky, gin, sherry.
Alcohol too speeds up the heart, makes the blood flow faster, and makes
more go to the skin. This makes the person feel hotter and livelier, so
small amounts are even good for the circulation. But when people have

too many drinks, it is quite different. First, their thinking becomes mud-
dled, they get confused, their feelings get out of control, they might
even get very angry or very sad, they can no longer think straight. Next,
their speech gets muddled: they cannot find the right words, or even say
them properly. And lastly, they can no longer walk, or even stand up any
more.
At the beginning of this book we looked at the upright walk of the
human being, and how it is the spirit in us which wants us to stand up-
right. As little toddlers we struggle to do this wonderful thing, to stand
upright and walk. (Even if someone, because of illness or injury, cannot
walk, the spirit still lives in them just the same). And as little children we
also learn to speak and we learn to think. Again, it is our own spirit which
makes us struggle to learn these things. Just imagine how it would be if
you could not stand up and walk, if you could not speak, if you could not
think. And now we see that too much alcohol works against the spirit in
us which taught us to stand up straight. Some people drink alcohol to
show how grown up they are, but all too often they show quite the
opposite.
We saw that all life on earth ultimately depends on the sun. It is the
sun that gives the warmth and energy for the plants to transform dead
mineral substance into nourishing food for animals and human beings.
And just as the sun is what gives us strength from outside, so our striving
spirit is what gives us inner strength to become capable and responsible
human beings.
