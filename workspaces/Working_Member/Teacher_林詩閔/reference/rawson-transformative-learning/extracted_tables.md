## Table 7

| Mode of recall | Notes: age and context related aspects |
| Embodied: Imitation/mimesis | Imitation is not always simultaneous and can occur sometime after experiencing another person or indeed any experience such as nature. Mimesis (the ancient Greek word for imitation) in art is ‘imitating’ or expressing a response to a nature mood in artistic expression, such as poetry, storytelling of any other form of performance, making it also a form of interpretation. It can also refer to the representation of experiences. Some theorists such as Walter Benjamin see mimesis as primal, pre-linguistic, authentic way of responding to the world, in many ways similar to the idea of affect as used today. It is a sensory basis for understanding. Imitation happens in all age groups, though it is a predominant mode of learning in younger children |
| Emulation (action) | Enacting something or someone that has been experienced. It is ‘being like’ rather than imitating or copying. |
| Locational memory | Certain places that have important to a person (or community) prompt memories. Visual or other sensory (e.g. smell, taste) references that call certain past experience sin to consciousness. Symbols can have a similar effect. The Ars memoriae involve mnemonic techniques that enable recall of texts or complex knowledge by drawing on retrieval clues. |
| Situational,  contextual or episodic recall | Like locational memory, certain situations call forth memories of experiences associated with the situation. This can also involve constructing mental models of experiences using various items, such as people, objects, settings and sequences of events. This shows that memory is not simply the retention of fixed representations of experiences but often of experiences embedded in situations with strong emotional associations. Situation memory occurs early in children’s lives |
| Procedural, implicit  memory and recall | Procedural memory is often unconscious, habitual and therefore automatic knowing how things are or were done and is linked to skills. This usually does not require conscious recall but simply (re)performing the skill. |
| Declarative memory and recall | Declarative memory is conscious recalls of facts and events (knowing what happened) and linked to knowledge. If we think of skills as knowledgeable action with purpose, then useful skill involves both what we recall, and how we can apply it for a specific and meaningful purpose. |
| Selective recall: Specific criteria,  Subjective objective | When we ask students to recall we can simply ask, “what do you recall?”. This is very general and requires quite high levels of motivation to reconstruction whole situations or stories. It is often more effective to target specific aspects that ask about details. One can ask questions about subjective feelings about past events of experiences, or one ask more ‘objective’ questions about exactly what was seen, heard or read. Waldorf education assumes that children can only begin to systematically recall events or experiences at will after the age of 6/7. After grade 1 they can gradually be led to expand their powers of recall. |
| Reporting: Direct and indirect  (with interpretation) | Reporting means recalling events or what someone said. This can be done be trying to reconstruct exactly what was said, or indirectly by summarizing what was said or seen in more general terms, which requires making judgments and taking a stance (did I believe the speaker? Or reporting what appeared to be going on, though it was unclear for some reason. |
